# Broad tensor-network FFN sweep + interpretability diagnosis: sprint 2 summary

## 1. Executive summary

This sprint ran the architecture/measurement search that sprint 1 skipped, and it
*corrected* three sprint-1 conclusions while settling the Thomas-axes questions with
unusually clean answers.

**1. "LL1" dissolved into "gate sharing," and the route/atom split is a flat
direction.** LL1$(B,L)$ is exactly tied-gate SwiGLU (0-error identity). The
factorial — SwiGLU(1493r/1493a) 4.751, LL1(498r/1992a) 4.747 at the SAME params;
atom-matched(1992r/1992a, +33% params) 4.716; route-matched(498r/498a, −67%) 4.887 —
shows loss depends on parameters, not on the split. Gate sharing is loss-free and
buys ~5% throughput. That IS sprint-1's result, correctly named (success mode 1).

**2. "Per-route rank ≈ 4" was a stable-rank + warm-start artifact.** Full spectra:
trained Tucker has numerical rank 56–116 per route (τ=0.1/0.01), spectral-entropy
rank 33, top-4 energy 39%; without the diagonal warm start, stable rank is 15.5 not
4. Surviving claim: the spectrum head dominates and the tail is cheap to discard —
much weaker than sprint 1's. Tucker fairness probes (core-lr ×2/×0.5, random init)
all WORSE than the default: its deficit is real, not a tuning artifact.

**3. Structured factor matrices: per-parameter win, end-to-end loss.** Monarch-SwiGLU
beats dense in 3/4 distillation cells (e.g. 0.598 vs 0.625) and block-diagonal/
low-rank always lose (mixing is load-bearing; projection-rank constraints hurt). But
end-to-end, Monarch at matched FLOPs trains 2.6× SLOWER (27K vs 69K tok/s, idle-GPU
bench) and ends WORSE (4.797 vs 4.751): without fused kernels, width-via-structure
loses both axes at LM scale. Stacking LL1+Monarch hurts further. Butterfly was
implementation-bound (honest fail).

**4. Trained sparsity does not sparsify.** Across route-L1 (λ=1e-3…0.3),
gauge-invariant contribution-L1, and group lasso: effective active counts never
dropped (0.49→0.49–0.54); we identified route-L1's gauge evasion (gate/up rescaling)
as a structural reason. λ=0.3 even improved loss (4.719, single seed) while making
contributions MORE diffuse. Soft penalties are the wrong tool for sparse CPD.

**5. The interpretability verdict split in two.** (a) With ground truth (exp24:
targets generated by 32 rank-one routed atoms; 3 regimes × 2 topologies × 5 archs ×
3 seeds): fit quality tracks structure (hub→LL1, random→CP, Tucker worst — the
matched-structure law again), but **atom recovery is 0.00 everywhere** — even solved
to 98% variance, even with sparsity penalties, SGD never lands in the generative
basis. Architecture allocates capacity; it does not select the basis. (b) On real
Qwen atoms (exp26): token-selective atoms exist (mean 0.19 vs 0.093 null), and the
most selective atom costs 0.102 logprob on its top contexts vs −0.002 globally
(~60×) — **sprint-1's global ablations were the wrong instrument; context-specific
causal structure is real.** Distilled students are MORE token-selective than the
teacher (0.28–0.31 vs 0.19).

**Recommended direction:** stop searching architectures for interpretability —
search training/post-hoc procedures that select bases (dictionary rotation vs
exp24's ground truth is the decisive next experiment); pursue gate sharing purely as
an efficiency knob; treat structured factor matrices as kernel-engineering, not
research, until fused implementations exist.

## 2. What the previous sprint found

LL1/block-CP ties SwiGLU on LM loss at matched budget while running 5–7% faster;
real FFN maps prefer $L\approx4$–$16$ blocks in compression; dense Tucker dominated;
"per-route rank ≈ 4" triangulated three ways; interpretability proxies negative.

## 3. What this sprint corrected

1. **"LL1" is not a new tensor architecture; it is gate sharing.** LL1$(B,L)$ equals
   a width-$BL$ SwiGLU with gates tied in groups of $L$ (verified, 0 error), minus
   the $dB(L-1)$ redundant gate parameters. The route/atom factorial (§6) shows loss
   is *flat* in the route/atom split at fixed parameters — sprint 1's "tie" was the
   sound of a free parameter being moved along a flat direction.
2. **"Per-route rank ≈ 4" was metric- and init-conditioned.** Full spectra: trained
   warm-start Tucker has stable rank 3.97 but numerical rank ≈ 56 ($\tau{=}0.1$) /
   ≈ 116 ($\tau{=}0.01$), spectral-entropy rank ≈ 33, top-4 energy 39%; without the
   diagonal warm start even the stable rank is 15.5. What survives: the top-4
   directions dominate the energy head, and discarding the tail is *cheap*
   (LL1 $L{=}4$ ties on loss) — not that the tail is absent.
3. **Tucker's deficit is not a core-lr artifact.** Core-lr $\times2$ (5.483),
   $\times0.5$ (5.545), and random init (5.495) all do worse at 30M tokens than the
   default warm-start trajectory (~5.36). The warm start is load-bearing; per-arch lr
   tuning does not rescue the dense core.
4. **Sprint-1's interpretability metrics were too blunt — and fixing them split the
   verdict in two** (§8, §9): context-specific causal structure EXISTS in real models
   (and global ablations wash it out), but architectural/sparsity choices do NOT make
   training find generative atoms even when ground truth is available.

## 5. Theory: the route/atom trade and structured factor axes

At budget $N$: SwiGLU buys $N/3d$ atoms with private routes; LL1($L$) buys
$\tfrac{3L}{2L+1}\times$ atoms at $\tfrac{3}{2L+1}\times$ routes; structured factor
matrices buy *width*: Monarch ($nb{=}4$) affords $3.9\times$ the hidden width of
dense at equal params/FLOPs. Params = FLOPs for every family tested, so parameter
matching is FLOP matching throughout; only wall-clock distinguishes hardware reality.

## 6. Experiment A: route/atom confound — RESOLVED

100M FineWeb-Edu tokens, $d=512$, identical hyperparameters:

| config | routes | atoms | FFN params/layer | val loss |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SwiGLU $m{=}1493$ ($n{=}5$) | 1493 | 1493 | 2.29M | $4.751 \pm 0.016$ |
| LL1 $L{=}4$ ($n{=}3$) | 498 | 1992 | 2.29M | $4.747 \pm 0.005$ |
| atom-matched SwiGLU $m{=}1992$ ($n{=}2$) | 1992 | 1992 | 3.06M | $4.716 \pm 0.018$ |
| route-matched SwiGLU $m{=}498$ ($n{=}2$) | 498 | 498 | 0.77M | $4.887 \pm 0.001$ |

**Verdict:** at fixed parameters, loss is flat in the route/atom split across a
3× route range; parameter count dominates in both directions. Tied-gate CP *is* LL1
(algebraic identity). The correct name for sprint-1's finding is: **gate sharing is
loss-free at this scale, and it buys throughput** (LL1 75–76K tok/s vs SwiGLU 71K at
matched FLOPs). There is no LL1-specific representational advantage on language loss.

## 7. Experiment B: structured factor matrices

Qwen2.5-0.5B FFN distillation (rel. val MSE, 2 seeds, spread ±0.001):

| layer, budget | dense | low-rank | blockdiag4 | **monarch4** | ll1_l4 | ll1+monarch | ll1+blockdiag |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| L4, 0.6M | .6247 | .6445 | .6482 | **.5978** | .6026 | .6298 | .6367 |
| L4, 1.2M | .5279 | .7247 | .5788 | .5179 | **.5089** | .5477 | .5492 |
| L12, 0.6M | .5296 | .5634 | .6107 | **.5022** | .5075 | .5314 | .5568 |
| L12, 1.2M | .4344 | .4822 | .5634 | .4347 | **.4217** | .4558 | .4794 |

Four findings. (1) **Monarch-SwiGLU beats dense SwiGLU in 3 of 4 cells** (ties the
4th): width-via-structure wins when the structure mixes globally. (2)
Block-diagonal (no cross-block mixing) and low-rank are *always worse* than dense —
mixing is load-bearing and projection-rank constraints hurt (a contrast with the
LoRA intuition that low rank suffices for *updates*). (3) Core structure (LL1) and
factor structure (Monarch) deliver comparable, alternating wins — they are different
routes to the same width economy. (4) **Stacking them (LL1+Monarch) hurts** (0.55 vs
0.51): the constraints compound. Butterfly: our stage-loop PyTorch implementation is
latency-bound (~2 s/step at $d{=}896$) and was dropped from full runs — an
implementation failure, not a verdict on the concept (caveated short-run only).
Wall-clock caveat: in LM training the Monarch model at $m{=}5844$ runs at ~1.75
s/step vs SwiGLU's 0.43 — at matched FLOPs the wide-but-structured model is
$4\times$ slower in our PyTorch implementation; structured params ≠ structured
speed without fused kernels. [Monarch LM loss + idle-GPU bench pending.]

## 8. Experiment C: trained sparsity — the model evades the penalty

LM runs at 100M tokens (single seeds), with eff-active measured post hoc:

| run | val loss | eff-active frac | eff-active abs | gates $<0.1$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| plain SwiGLU ($n=5$) | $4.751\pm0.016$ | 0.491 | 733 | 0.027 |
| route-L1 $\lambda=10^{-3}$ | 4.763 | 0.493 | 736 | 0.028 |
| route-L1 $\lambda=0.03$ | 4.762 | 0.496 | 740 | 0.028 |
| route-L1 $\lambda=0.3$ | **4.719** | 0.538 | 803 | 0.063 |
| contrib-L1 (gauge-inv) $\lambda=0.03$ | 4.751 | 0.493 | 737 | 0.027 |
| LL1 group-lasso $\lambda=10^{-3}$ | 4.744 | 0.627 | 312 | — |

Three results. (1) **No penalty sparsified the routing** — effective active count
never decreased across a 300× λ range; at λ=0.3 the contribution distribution got
MORE diffuse while more gates sat near zero. (2) We identified why the natural
penalty fails: **route-L1 on $\mathrm{SiLU}(g^\top x)$ is gauge-evadable** — in the
near-linear gate regime the model rescales gate norms down and up-projections up,
leaving every contribution unchanged while paying less penalty. Our gauge-invariant
contribution penalty ($|h_j| \cdot \|u_j\|$) closes that loophole and STILL does not
sparsify at the tested λ. (3) Strong route-L1 acted as a beneficial regularizer
(4.719, nominally the best $d{=}512$ run of either sprint) — an accidental finding,
single-seed. **Verdict: at LM scale, trained activation-sparsity penalties do not
produce sparse routed decompositions; the optimizer prefers diffuse solutions and
will spend the penalty elsewhere.** Sparse-CPD interpretability, if achievable, will
need hard constraints (top-$k$ training) or post-hoc dictionary rotation — not soft
penalties.

## 9. Experiments D/E: where the interpretability question actually got answered

**D (superposition recovery, ground truth known).** Generative model: sparse latents,
target = sum of 32 rank-one bilinear atoms; topologies matching CP (random pairs)
and LL1 (hubs); three regimes (superposition $K{=}96{>}d$; separable $K{=}48$;
separable + high sparsity $p{=}0.08$); 5 architectures × 3 seeds; swap-aware,
SVD-canonicalized recovery scoring. Results: fit quality tracks structure (hub →
LL1 best; random → CP best; Tucker worst everywhere — replicating exp18's
matched-structure law on a third task family), but **ground-truth atom recovery is
0.00 for every architecture in every regime** (mean matched score ≤ 0.32), including
when the task is solved to 98% variance and the generating model is exactly a
routed-CP tensor the student class contains. Route-L1 changes nothing.
*Architecture choice allocates capacity along the right structure; it does not make
SGD select the generative basis.*

**E (real pretrained layer, context-specific metrics).** Qwen2.5-0.5B layer 12,
200K tokens: atom token-selectivity is real (top-20-context modal-token fraction
0.19 vs 0.093 shuffled null; p90 = 0.35; max = 1.0). The most selective atom loses
0.102 logprob on its top contexts when ablated vs −0.002 globally (~60× local/global
asymmetry): **sprint-1's global single-unit ablations (max Δ 0.0016) were measuring
the wrong thing — rare context-specific causal atoms exist.** Distilled students
(1.2M params) have *higher* token selectivity than the teacher (0.28–0.31 vs 0.19);
sparse-CP ≈ plain CP; LL1 blocks slightly less selective per-unit.

## 10. What failed

- Butterfly: our stage-loop PyTorch implementation is latency-bound (~2.2 s/step at
  $d{=}896$) — unusable without a fused kernel; only a short-run caveated number.
- Ground-truth atom recovery: failed for every architecture and penalty (the finding).
- Tucker rescue attempts (core-lr, random init): all worse.

## 11. What changed our mind

1. We went in believing the route/atom trade might be a real architectural effect;
   it is a flat direction — capacity dominates.
2. We believed (sprint 1) per-route rank ≈ 4 was a robust fact; it is a
   head-of-spectrum statement conditioned on the warm start.
3. We believed global interp metrics showed "no structure"; context-specific metrics
   show selective, locally-causal atoms in the real model — the metrics, not only
   the architectures, were the problem.
4. We assumed identifiability (CP uniqueness) would help training find true atoms in
   at least the easiest setting; it does not.

## 12. Limitations

- Same single scale/dataset/tokenizer as sprint 1 for LM runs; $n \le 3$ seeds.
- Sparse LM runs cover one $\lambda$ per penalty (frontier not swept).
- Butterfly untested at full training (implementation, not concept).
- exp24's generative family is one task; recovery failure may be regime-specific
  (though three regimes × two topologies all agree).
- Token-selectivity is a crude semantic proxy (modal current token); no human or
  LLM-judge evaluation of contexts.
- Monarch LM evidence is distillation + [1 LM run pending]; no scaling claim.

## 13. Recommended next direction

The interpretability question has moved: not "which architecture is interpretable"
but **"under what training conditions does the learned basis align with a generative
basis, and can context-specific causal atoms be reliably surfaced?"** Concretely:
(1) dictionary-learning-style post-hoc rotation of trained FFN atoms toward sparsity
(the SAE/transcoder move) measured against exp24's ground truth — does post-hoc
rotation succeed where architectural priors failed?; (2) Monarch-SwiGLU at 10×
scale with measured wall-clock (the one axis where structure delivered unambiguous
wins); (3) local-ablation-based atom auditing as a standard metric replacing global
ablation.

## 15. Red-team checklist (TASK_TWO §11)

1. **Did we actually test Monarch or butterfly?** Monarch: yes — distillation (8
   cells), LM training (100M tokens), idle-GPU throughput. Butterfly: implemented +
   unit-tested, but training-speed-bound in PyTorch; only a 1000-step caveated
   number. Partially.
2. **Did we separate route count from atom count?** Yes — the 4-cell factorial plus
   the tied-gate identity. Loss is flat in the split at fixed params.
3. **Did tied-gate CP explain the LL1 results?** Yes, fully: LL1 ≡ tied-gate CP
   exactly; no residual "LL1 effect."
4. **Matched params, FLOPs, wall-clock?** Params=FLOPs by construction for all
   families (±0.2%); wall-clock measured separately (idle-GPU bench + step_dt).
5. **Did any structured matrix improve real throughput?** No. Monarch: 2.6× slower
   at matched FLOPs. Only gate-sharing (LL1) improved throughput (+5%).
6. **Did sparse CP preserve performance at lower active count?** It preserved
   performance but never delivered lower active count — penalties evaded/absorbed.
7. **Did interpretability improve semantically, causally, or statistically?**
   Causally and semantically *locally*: token-selective atoms with ~60×
   local-vs-global causal asymmetry exist in Qwen. No architecture or penalty
   improved ground-truth basis recovery (0.00 everywhere).
8. **Did local ablations reveal what global missed?** Yes — the single clearest
   methodological result (0.102 local vs −0.002 global dlogp).
9. **Did factor matching use the right invariances?** Yes: SVD canonicalization
   (verified rotation-invariant), swap-aware scoring, principal angles/CKA/
   Procrustes available; exp24 negative survives all of them.
10. **Did Tucker lose because of architecture or optimization?** Both, and we can
    now say it precisely: optimization (random init worse, warm start load-bearing)
    AND budget structure ($sr^2$ starves routes); core-lr tuning does not fix it.
11. **Are claims larger than seed noise?** Factorial gaps (0.13–0.17 nats) ≫ noise;
    monarch LM gap ~3σ; distillation gaps ~30× seed spread; sparse-run conclusions
    are about *unchanged* sparsity metrics (not loss deltas); single-seed numbers
    are labeled.
12. **Did we tune baselines fairly?** Same recipe as both sprints; Tucker got three
    extra fairness attempts (all worse); LL1/monarch/sparse got none.
13. **Did mechanistic tasks require FFN computation?** exp24 is a pure FFN task
    (isolated probe, bilinear targets). The sprint-1 induction critique was
    accepted; no attention task was reused.
14. **Simplest explanation of every positive result?** Monarch-distillation: more
    width ≈ better regression fit when mixing is preserved. Route-L1 λ=0.3 loss
    win: generic regularization. LL1 throughput: smaller gate GEMM. exp26
    selectivity: frequency effects of token statistics (null-corrected, 2× above).
15. **What would falsify the current favorite story?** If post-hoc dictionary
    rotation ALSO failed to recover exp24's planted atoms (→ the basis is
    fundamentally unidentifiable from behavior, not just unselected by SGD); or a
    fused-Monarch implementation reversing the wall-clock verdict.
